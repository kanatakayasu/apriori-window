\begin{abstract}
Dense interval detection in frequent pattern mining identifies temporal periods where itemset support exceeds a threshold.
However, real-world data often possesses additional dimensions---spatial location, customer demographics, product category---where dense regions form irregular shapes rather than simple intervals.
We introduce the \emph{support surface}, a multi-dimensional generalization of the support time series, and define \emph{dense regions} as connected components of its superlevel set.
We prove the \emph{Dense Region Containment Theorem}, which extends the Apriori anti-monotonicity property to arbitrary dimensions and enables candidate region pruning.
We further formalize \emph{dimension decomposability}, providing sufficient conditions under which the multi-dimensional problem decomposes into independent 1D sub-problems.
We present the \emph{Sweep Surface} algorithm that combines prefix-sum computation with union-find connected component labeling, achieving $O(G \cdot \alpha(G))$ complexity where $G$ is the grid size.
Experiments on synthetic and semi-realistic retail datasets demonstrate:
(1) perfect recovery of planted dense regions,
(2) up to 2$\times$ speedup of sweep surface over na\"ive enumeration,
(3) scalability to million-cell grids in 3D, and
(4) discovery of interpretable spatiotemporal patterns in simulated retail scenarios.
\end{abstract}

\keywords{dense region mining, support surface, multi-dimensional patterns, sweep algorithm, superlevel sets}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
% ==============================================================

Frequent pattern mining~\cite{agrawal1994apriori,han2000mining} is a cornerstone of data mining, identifying itemsets that appear together frequently in transaction databases.
While most work assumes a single aggregate support threshold, real-world patterns exhibit temporal variation---a product bundle may be popular during a holiday season but rare otherwise.
The \emph{dense interval} framework addresses this by computing a support time series and identifying contiguous temporal windows where support exceeds a threshold.

However, many practical settings involve multiple contextual dimensions beyond time.
A retail chain may observe that a product combination is popular during summer (temporal) in urban stores (spatial) but not in rural ones.
Epidemiological surveillance seeks space-time clusters of disease incidence.
In these settings, the relevant concept is not a 1D interval but a multi-dimensional \emph{dense region}: an arbitrarily shaped connected area in the space of contextual dimensions where pattern support is elevated.

Existing approaches address related but distinct problems.
\textbf{Spatial scan statistics}~\cite{kulldorff1997spatial,kulldorff2001prospective} detect clusters in point processes but are restricted to circular or cylindrical scanning windows and do not handle itemset patterns.
\textbf{Dense subtensor mining}~\cite{shin2017densealert,shin2018mzoom} finds dense blocks in multi-dimensional arrays but only axis-aligned rectangles, missing irregular region shapes.
\textbf{Density-based clustering}~\cite{ester1996dbscan,ankerst1999optics} discovers clusters of arbitrary shape in point data, but operates on raw data points rather than derived support functions.
\textbf{Co-location pattern mining}~\cite{shekhar2001colocation} identifies spatially associated feature types but lacks temporal dynamics and the notion of a continuous support surface.

We bridge these gaps by introducing the \emph{support surface}---a function $S_P(t, \mathbf{v})$ that counts the support of pattern $P$ within a sliding window at each point $(t, \mathbf{v})$ in the multi-dimensional domain.
Dense regions are then naturally defined as connected components of the superlevel set $\{(t, \mathbf{v}) : S_P(t, \mathbf{v}) \geq \theta\}$.
This formulation generalizes 1D dense intervals, connects to topological data analysis via persistent homology~\cite{edelsbrunner2010computational}, and enables efficient algorithms based on sweep techniques from computational geometry~\cite{shamos1976geometric}.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
\item We formalize the \textbf{support surface} and \textbf{dense region} concepts for multi-dimensional transaction data (Section~\ref{sec:problem}).

\item We prove the \textbf{Dense Region Containment Theorem}, extending Apriori anti-monotonicity to multi-dimensional regions, enabling pruning during mining (Section~\ref{sec:problem}).

\item We introduce \textbf{dimension decomposability}, a property that enables factoring the multi-dimensional problem into independent 1D sub-problems when the support surface is approximately separable (Section~\ref{sec:problem}).

\item We present the \textbf{Sweep Surface algorithm} with $O(G \cdot \alpha(G))$ complexity for dense region extraction, where $G$ is the grid size and $\alpha$ is the inverse Ackermann function (Section~\ref{sec:method}).

\item We conduct comprehensive experiments demonstrating effectiveness, scalability, and practical utility (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Proposed Method}\label{sec:method}
% ==============================================================

We present two algorithms for solving Problem~\ref{prob:main}: the \emph{Sweep Surface} algorithm for the general case and a \emph{Decomposed} algorithm when the support surface is approximately separable.

\subsection{Support Surface Computation}

\paragraph{Na\"ive approach.}
For each grid point $(t, \mathbf{v})$, iterate over all transactions in the window $[t, t+W_0) \times \mathrm{Box}(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{W})$ and count those containing pattern $P$.
This requires $O(G \cdot N)$ time, which is prohibitive for large datasets.

\paragraph{Prefix-sum approach.}
We first build a $(k{+}1)$-dimensional indicator array $I$ where $I[t, \mathbf{x}(t)]$ counts transactions at grid position $(t, \mathbf{x}(t))$ that contain $P$.
We then compute prefix sums along each dimension.
The support at any grid point is obtained by inclusion-exclusion on $2^{k+1}$ corners of the prefix sum array, requiring $O(G \cdot 2^{k+1} + N)$ total time.

\subsection{Dense Region Extraction}

Given the support surface $S_P$, we extract dense regions using Algorithm~\ref{alg:sweep}.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Sweep Surface Dense Region Detection}
\label{alg:sweep}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Support surface $S_P$, threshold $\theta$, grid shape $(T_0, T_1, \ldots, T_k)$
\ENSURE Set of dense regions $\mathcal{R}_\theta(P)$
\STATE Initialize UnionFind $U$ with $G = \prod_j T_j$ elements
\STATE $\textit{above} \leftarrow \emptyset$
\FOR{$t = 0$ to $T_0 - 1$}
    \FOR{each $\mathbf{v}$ in slice at position $t$}
        \IF{$S_P(t, \mathbf{v}) \geq \theta$}
            \STATE $f \leftarrow \textsc{FlatIndex}(t, \mathbf{v})$
            \STATE $\textit{above} \leftarrow \textit{above} \cup \{f\}$
            \FOR{each grid neighbor $\mathbf{n}$ of $(t, \mathbf{v})$}
                \STATE $f_n \leftarrow \textsc{FlatIndex}(\mathbf{n})$
                \IF{$f_n \in \textit{above}$}
                    \STATE $U.\textsc{Union}(f, f_n)$
                \ENDIF
            \ENDFOR
        \ENDIF
    \ENDFOR
\ENDFOR
\STATE \textbf{return} connected components of $U$ restricted to \textit{above}
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{Complexity.}
Each grid cell is visited once ($O(G)$).
For each cell above threshold, we check $2(k+1)$ neighbors.
Each union-find operation is $O(\alpha(G))$.
Total: $O(G \cdot (k+1) \cdot \alpha(G))$.
Since $k$ is typically small (2--3) and $\alpha$ is effectively constant, the practical complexity is $O(G)$.

\subsection{Multi-Dimensional Apriori Mining}

The full mining pipeline (Algorithm~\ref{alg:mining}) uses Apriori-style level-wise search with dense region containment pruning.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Multi-Dimensional Dense Itemset Mining}
\label{alg:mining}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Transaction data $D$, window sizes $\mathbf{W}$, threshold $\theta$, max length $L_{\max}$
\ENSURE All patterns $P$ with $\mathcal{R}_\theta(P) \neq \emptyset$
\STATE $\mathcal{F}_1 \leftarrow \emptyset$
\FOR{each item $i \in \mathcal{I}$}
    \STATE Compute $S_{\{i\}}$; extract $\mathcal{R}_\theta(\{i\})$
    \IF{$\mathcal{R}_\theta(\{i\}) \neq \emptyset$}
        \STATE $\mathcal{F}_1 \leftarrow \mathcal{F}_1 \cup \{\{i\}\}$
    \ENDIF
\ENDFOR
\FOR{$k = 2$ to $L_{\max}$}
    \STATE $\mathcal{C}_k \leftarrow \textsc{GenerateCandidates}(\mathcal{F}_{k-1}, k)$
    \STATE $\mathcal{C}_k \leftarrow \textsc{AprioriPrune}(\mathcal{C}_k, \mathcal{F}_{k-1})$
    \STATE $\mathcal{F}_k \leftarrow \emptyset$
    \FOR{each $P \in \mathcal{C}_k$}
        \STATE $\mathcal{R}_{\text{cand}} \leftarrow \bigcap_{Q \subset P, |Q|=k-1} \mathcal{L}_\theta(S_Q)$ \COMMENT{Cor.~\ref{cor:candidate_restriction}}
        \IF{$\mathcal{R}_{\text{cand}} = \emptyset$}
            \STATE \textbf{continue} \COMMENT{Pruned by Cor.~\ref{cor:pruning}}
        \ENDIF
        \STATE Compute $S_P$ restricted to $\mathcal{R}_{\text{cand}}$
        \STATE Extract $\mathcal{R}_\theta(P)$
        \IF{$\mathcal{R}_\theta(P) \neq \emptyset$}
            \STATE $\mathcal{F}_k \leftarrow \mathcal{F}_k \cup \{P\}$
        \ENDIF
    \ENDFOR
\ENDFOR
\STATE \textbf{return} $\bigcup_k \mathcal{F}_k$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Decomposition-Aware Variant}

When the support surface is approximately dimension-decomposable (Definition~\ref{def:decomposable}), we can exploit Theorem~\ref{thm:decomposition} for significant speedup.
Our adaptive algorithm first checks decomposability via rank-1 tensor approximation:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Compute the rank-1 approximation $\hat{S} = f_0 \otimes f_1 \otimes \cdots \otimes f_k$ (via SVD for 2D, ALS for higher dimensions).
\item If $\|S - \hat{S}\|_F / \|S\|_F < \epsilon$, use the decomposed algorithm (solve $k{+}1$ independent 1D problems).
\item Otherwise, fall back to the full sweep surface algorithm.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{RQ1}: Can the algorithm recover planted dense regions? (E1)
\textbf{RQ2}: What is the accuracy-speed tradeoff of dimension decomposition? (E2)
\textbf{RQ3}: How does the algorithm scale? (E3)
\textbf{RQ4}: Can it discover meaningful patterns in realistic settings? (E4)

\subsection{E1: Planted Dense Region Recovery}

\paragraph{Setup.}
We generate 2D synthetic data (200 transactions, 20 spatial positions, 10 items) with planted dense regions of varying configurations: single block, two separate blocks, overlapping patterns, and weak signal.
Window sizes are $(W_0, W_1) = (10, 5)$ with threshold $\theta = 3$.

\subsection{E2: Dimension Decomposition}

\paragraph{Setup.}
We construct 2D support surfaces ($50 \times 30$) with controlled decomposability: a rank-1 base with varying levels of interaction noise (0\%, 30\%, 70\% of mean value).
We measure the rank-1 approximation error and compare region detection accuracy.

\paragraph{Results.}
Table~\ref{tab:e2} shows that the decomposition error faithfully reflects the interaction strength.
Separable surfaces achieve 1.6\% error; weak interaction 12.6\%; strong interaction 27.8\%.
The sweep surface algorithm achieves Jaccard = 1.0 with ground truth in all cases, confirming correctness regardless of decomposability.

\subsection{E4: Semi-Realistic Retail Scenario}

\paragraph{Setup.}
We simulate a retail dataset: 365 days, 20 stores, 50 items, with three planted event types (summer products, winter products, promotional items).
We mine dense itemsets with window $(W_0, W_1) = (30, 5)$ and threshold $\theta = 5$.

\paragraph{Results.}
The algorithm discovers 4 multi-item patterns, all corresponding to planted events.
The winter pattern \{5, 6, 7\} is detected with 58 dense regions spanning 1,873 grid cells, correctly capturing its seasonal and regional structure.
Total mining time is 0.53 seconds for 2,000 transactions.

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}
% ==============================================================

\paragraph{Spatial scan statistics.}
Kulldorff~\cite{kulldorff1997spatial} introduced the spatial scan statistic for detecting clusters in point processes using circular scanning windows.
Space-time extensions~\cite{kulldorff2001prospective} use cylindrical windows.
Scalable variants~\cite{matheny2016scalable,neill2012fast} address computational challenges.
Unlike these approaches, our method works with \emph{itemset patterns} rather than point events, supports \emph{arbitrary region shapes} rather than geometric primitives, and leverages Apriori-style pruning for multi-pattern mining.

\paragraph{Dense subtensor mining.}
DenseAlert~\cite{shin2017densealert}, M-Zoom~\cite{shin2018mzoom}, and D-Cube~\cite{shin2017dcube} detect dense blocks in multi-dimensional tensors for anomaly detection.
These methods find \emph{axis-aligned} dense blocks, whereas our dense regions can have arbitrary shapes as connected components of superlevel sets.

\paragraph{Density-based clustering.}
DBSCAN~\cite{ester1996dbscan} and OPTICS~\cite{ankerst1999optics} find clusters of arbitrary shape in point data.
Our approach operates on a derived \emph{function} (support surface) rather than raw data points, enabling pattern-specific dense region detection.
The conceptual connection is through level sets: DBSCAN uses point density, while we use windowed support as the density function.

\paragraph{Co-location pattern mining.}
Shekhar and Huang~\cite{shekhar2001colocation} formalized spatial co-location patterns with Apriori-based mining.
Our Dense Region Containment Theorem (Theorem~\ref{thm:containment}) provides the multi-dimensional analog of their anti-monotone participation index, but operates on continuous support surfaces rather than discrete spatial relationships.

\paragraph{Topological data analysis.}
The characterization of dense regions as connected components of superlevel sets connects to persistent homology~\cite{edelsbrunner2010computational,carlsson2009topology}.
Varying the threshold $\theta$ induces a filtration whose persistence diagram encodes the robustness of dense regions.
We leave the integration of persistence-based analysis as future work.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
% ==============================================================

We have introduced a framework for multi-dimensional dense region mining that generalizes dense interval detection from one dimension to arbitrary dimensions.
The key insight is to view pattern support as a function---the \emph{support surface}---over the product of all contextual dimensions, and to define dense regions as connected components of its superlevel set.

Our theoretical contributions include the Dense Region Containment Theorem (enabling Apriori pruning in multi-dimensional space) and the Dimension Decomposability Theorem (enabling factored computation when dimensions are approximately independent).
The Sweep Surface algorithm achieves near-linear complexity in grid size and scales to million-cell grids.

Experiments demonstrate perfect planted region recovery, meaningful scalability improvements, and practical applicability to retail spatiotemporal mining.

\paragraph{Future work.}
(1) Integration with persistent homology for multi-threshold analysis.
(2) Online/streaming variants for real-time surveillance.
(3) Extension to non-grid domains (e.g., graph-structured spatial dimensions).
(4) Application to large-scale epidemiological datasets.
