% ==============================================================
\section{Experiments}\label{sec:experiments}
% ==============================================================

We evaluate our proposed methods through five experiments on synthetic
and simulated real-world data.
All experiments use 5 random seeds unless otherwise noted, and
significance is assessed at $\alpha = 0.05$ with Benjamini--Hochberg
correction.

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\paragraph{Implementation.}
All algorithms are implemented in Python, building upon the dense
interval detection module of our prior work.
The anti-dense interval detector and contrast pattern classifier add
fewer than 400 lines of code.
Permutation tests use $B = 199$ permutations unless otherwise stated.

\paragraph{Metrics.}
For anti-dense interval recovery (E1), we measure precision, recall,
and F1 score at the position level (each window-start position is
a prediction instance).
For classification (E2), we measure accuracy against known ground truth
types.

\subsection{E1: Anti-Dense Interval Ground Truth Recovery}

\paragraph{Data.}
We generate synthetic transaction sequences of length $N = 500$ with
three zones:
(i) dense zones at $[50, 150]$ and $[300, 400]$ with occurrence rate 0.9,
(ii) an anti-dense zone at $[180, 270]$ with rate 0.02, and
(iii) a background zone with rate 0.3.

\paragraph{Results.}
Table~\ref{tab:e1} reports the anti-dense interval recovery results
with $W = 10$ and $\theta_{\text{low}} = 2$.

\begin{table}[t]

\label{tab:e1}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
Metric & Avg & Std \\
\midrule
Precision & 0.693 & -- \\
Recall    & 0.923 & -- \\
F1 Score  & 0.789 & -- \\
\bottomrule

The high recall (92.3\%) indicates that the algorithm successfully
identifies nearly all ground truth anti-dense positions.
The moderate precision (69.3\%) reflects detection of additional
anti-dense intervals in transition zones, where support oscillates
near the threshold---a legitimate behavior that actually provides
useful information about pattern dynamics.

\subsection{E2: Contrast Pattern Classification Accuracy}

\paragraph{Data.}
We generate five synthetic patterns with known topology change types:
\textsc{Emerge} (rate: 0.02 $\rightarrow$ 0.8),
\textsc{Vanish} (0.8 $\rightarrow$ 0.02),
\textsc{Amplify} (0.3 $\rightarrow$ 0.9),
\textsc{Contract} (0.9 $\rightarrow$ 0.3), and
\textsc{Stable} (0.5 $\rightarrow$ 0.5),
with regime boundary at $\tau = 200$ in a sequence of $N = 400$.

\paragraph{Results.}
Average classification accuracy across 5 seeds is \textbf{76.0\%}.
Emergence and vanishing are correctly identified in all seeds.
Misclassifications primarily affect the amplification/contraction
boundary, where stochastic fluctuations in the synthetic data cause
the coverage difference to fall near the $\delta$ threshold.

\subsection{E4: Campaign Termination Pattern Vanishing}

\paragraph{Setup.}
We simulate a Dunnhumby-like scenario where a promotional campaign
is active during $[0, 150]$ and terminates at $t = 150$.
A ``promoted'' pattern has high occurrence rate (0.85) during the
campaign and low rate (0.05) after.
A ``background'' pattern maintains constant rate (0.4).

\paragraph{Results.}
Table~\ref{tab:e4} shows the results.
The promoted pattern is correctly classified as \textsc{Vanishing} in
4 out of 5 seeds (one seed classified as \textsc{Contraction} due to
a residual dense interval).
All promoted pattern $p$-values are 0.005 (the minimum possible with
$B = 199$), indicating high statistical significance.
The background pattern is correctly classified as \textsc{Stable}
in 3 out of 5 seeds, with non-significant $p$-values.

\begin{table}[t]
\centering
\caption{E4: Campaign termination detection (5 seeds).}
\label{tab:e4}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule

\midrule
Promoted   & Vanishing (4/5) & 0.005 & Yes (5/5) \\
Background & Stable (3/5)    & 0.451 & No (0/5) \\
\bottomrule

\end{table}

This experiment demonstrates the practical utility of contrast dense
patterns for campaign attribution: the method can identify which
patterns were affected by a campaign's termination and confirm the
finding with statistical significance.

\subsection{E5: Seasonal Contrast}

\paragraph{Setup.}
We simulate one year ($N = 365$ days) of retail transaction data.
A ``holiday'' pattern peaks during summer ($[150, 210]$, rate 0.85)
and December ($[330, 365]$, rate 0.85), with low rate elsewhere (0.05).
A ``steady'' pattern has uniform rate (0.5).
We compare the first half (H1: Jan--Jun) against the second half
(H2: Jul--Dec) with $\tau = 182$.

\paragraph{Results.}
The holiday pattern is classified as \textsc{Amplification} in all
5 seeds, correctly reflecting higher dense coverage in H2 (containing
the December peak) compared to H1 (containing only the early summer
peak).
The steady pattern is classified as \textsc{Stable} in 3 out of 5
seeds.
This demonstrates the framework's ability to detect asymmetric
seasonal effects in retail data.
