\begin{abstract}
Pharmacovigilance relies on detecting changes in drug prescription patterns following regulatory safety communications. Existing methods---such as disproportionality analysis, self-controlled case series, and interrupted time series---operate on single drug--event pairs with manually specified intervention points. We propose a framework that combines sliding-window frequent itemset mining with automated regulatory event attribution to detect \emph{dense prescription intervals}: time periods during which co-prescription patterns become unusually frequent. Our method (1)~adapts the Apriori-window algorithm to ATC-coded prescription transactions, (2)~identifies change points in pattern density, and (3)~attributes these changes to regulatory events using Welch's $t$-test with Benjamini--Hochberg FDR control. Experiments on synthetic MIMIC-IV-style prescription data demonstrate that the framework detects 3{,}100 dense patterns across 19 ATC drug classes, identifies 856 disappearing and 601 emerging contrast patterns around simulated FDA safety communications, and achieves sub-second analysis on 1{,}000-transaction datasets. Cross-class analysis across cardiovascular, pain management, and antibiotic drug classes reveals differential response patterns, with opioid--benzodiazepine co-prescriptions showing the strongest decline following a simulated boxed warning. The framework provides automated, multi-pattern surveillance capabilities that complement existing pharmacovigilance methods.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} pharmacoepidemiology, dense interval mining, prescription pattern analysis, pharmacovigilance, regulatory event attribution, co-prescription mining

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Monitoring drug prescription patterns is a cornerstone of pharmacovigilance. When regulatory agencies such as the U.S.\ Food and Drug Administration (FDA) issue safety communications---including boxed warnings, safety alerts, and drug withdrawals---healthcare providers are expected to modify their prescribing behavior accordingly~\citep{dusetzina2012fda}. However, the impact of these communications varies considerably; some produce immediate, strong effects on prescribing, while others have delayed or no measurable impact~\citep{marcum2012fda}.

Current methods for assessing the impact of regulatory actions on prescription patterns face several limitations. \emph{Disproportionality analysis} methods such as the Proportional Reporting Ratio (PRR)~\citep{evans2001prr}, Reporting Odds Ratio (ROR), and Bayesian Confidence Propagation Neural Network (BCPNN)~\citep{bate1998bcpnn} operate on aggregate counts across the entire observation period and cannot capture temporal dynamics. \emph{Interrupted time series} (ITS) analysis~\citep{bernal2017its,wagner2002its} provides temporal assessment but requires manual specification of intervention points and typically analyzes one drug at a time. The \emph{self-controlled case series} (SCCS)~\citep{whitaker2006sccs,cadarette2021sccs} method controls for between-person confounding but is limited to transient exposures and individual-level analysis.

Meanwhile, data mining approaches have been applied to prescription data. Association rule mining has been used to identify polypharmacy patterns~\citep{carvalho2022apriori,pappa2017polypharmacy}, sequential pattern mining to discover ordered prescription sequences~\citep{wright2015sequential}, and temporal pattern discovery to detect drug--outcome associations~\citep{noren2010temporal,batal2012temporal}. However, none of these methods simultaneously addresses three critical requirements: (i)~detecting \emph{when} specific co-prescription patterns become unusually frequent, (ii)~handling \emph{multiple patterns} simultaneously, and (iii)~\emph{automatically} attributing detected changes to known regulatory events.

In this paper, we propose a framework that bridges frequent itemset mining and pharmacovigilance by introducing three methodological contributions:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Dense prescription interval detection}: We adapt the sliding-window Apriori algorithm to ATC-coded prescription transaction data, enabling automatic identification of time periods where co-prescription patterns become unusually dense.

    \item \textbf{Regulatory event attribution}: We formalize the problem of linking detected density changes to regulatory events using contrast pattern analysis with Welch's $t$-test and Benjamini--Hochberg FDR control.

    \item \textbf{Multi-pattern surveillance}: Unlike single-drug methods, our framework simultaneously detects disappearing patterns (targeted drugs declining), emerging patterns (alternative drugs increasing), and stable patterns (unaffected drug classes), providing a comprehensive view of prescribing behavior changes.
\end{enumerate}

We evaluate the framework on synthetic prescription data modeled after the MIMIC-IV electronic health record structure~\citep{johnson2023mimiciv}, demonstrating its ability to detect clinically meaningful pattern changes around simulated FDA safety communications.

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Pharmacovigilance Signal Detection}

Spontaneous adverse drug reaction (ADR) reporting systems form the basis of post-marketing drug safety surveillance. The primary analytical methods are disproportionality measures: PRR~\citep{evans2001prr} and ROR compare drug--event frequencies against background rates, while Bayesian methods including BCPNN~\citep{bate1998bcpnn} and the Gamma-Poisson Shrinker (GPS)~\citep{dumouchel1999gps} provide shrinkage-based estimates. Comparative studies~\citep{lee2020comparison} show that frequentist methods (PRR, ROR) tend to have higher sensitivity while Bayesian methods are more conservative. These methods operate on aggregate data and do not capture temporal variation in signal strength.

\subsection{Temporal Analysis in Pharmacoepidemiology}

Self-controlled designs~\citep{cadarette2021sccs,petersen2016selfcontrolled} compare exposed and unexposed periods within the same individual, controlling for time-stable confounders. SCCS methods require pre-specification of risk windows and are best suited for transient exposures with acute outcomes.

Interrupted time series (ITS) analysis~\citep{bernal2017its,jandoc2015its} is the predominant quasi-experimental design for evaluating regulatory interventions. Segmented regression is used in 67\% of ITS studies in drug utilization research~\citep{jandoc2015its}, though the method requires manual specification of intervention time points.

Prescription sequence symmetry analysis (PSSA)~\citep{hallas1996pssa,pratt2015pssa} detects temporal associations between drug initiations but does not address co-prescription density.

\subsection{Prescription Pattern Mining}

Association rule mining has been applied to polypharmacy analysis~\citep{carvalho2022apriori,pappa2017polypharmacy,doherty2020polypharmacy}, identifying common drug combinations in specific patient populations. Sequential pattern mining~\citep{wright2015sequential} discovers ordered medication sequences. Temporal pattern discovery~\citep{noren2010temporal,batal2012temporal,landi2020temporal,alonso2023temporal} identifies temporal associations in electronic health records.

Topic models~\citep{chen2017topic} and representation learning approaches~\citep{jensen2012ehr} have been applied to MIMIC data~\citep{johnson2023mimiciv,gupta2022pipeline} for medication pattern discovery. However, these approaches focus on static patterns or next-prescription prediction rather than temporal density variation.

\subsection{Positioning}

Our work differs from existing methods in combining (i)~sliding-window dense interval detection for co-prescription patterns, (ii)~automated attribution to regulatory events across multiple patterns, and (iii)~contrast pattern classification (disappearing vs.\ emerging vs.\ stable). \section{Method}\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Prescription Transaction Model}

\begin{definition}[Prescription Transaction]
Let $\mathcal{A}_\ell$ denote the set of ATC codes at hierarchy level $\ell \in \{1, \ldots, 5\}$. A \emph{prescription transaction} $d_t$ at time $t$ is the set of ATC codes prescribed to any patient during time period $t$:
\[
d_t = \{\phi_\ell(\text{drug}) : \text{drug} \in \text{Prescriptions}(t)\} \subseteq \mathcal{A}_\ell
\]
where $\phi_\ell$ truncates a drug's ATC code to level $\ell$. The transaction sequence is $D = [d_1, \ldots, d_N]$.
\end{definition}

We use ATC level 3 (pharmacological subgroup) to balance specificity and statistical power. Each time period may correspond to a day, week, or patient visit.

\subsection{Dense Prescription Interval Detection}

\begin{definition}[Support Time Series]
For pattern $P \subseteq \mathcal{A}_\ell$ and window size $W$:
\begin{equation}
s_P(t) = |\{d_j \in D : t \leq j < t + W, P \subseteq d_j\}|
\label{eq:support}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Dense Prescription Interval]
A \emph{dense prescription interval} for pattern $P$ with threshold $\theta$ is a maximal contiguous range $[s, e]$ such that $s_P(t) \geq \theta$ for all $t \in [s, e]$. The set of all such intervals is $\mathcal{D}(P, W, \theta)$.
\end{definition}

We enumerate dense prescription patterns using Algorithm~\ref{alg:dense_mining}, which extends the Apriori frequent itemset mining framework with sliding-window support counting and anti-monotone pruning.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Dense Prescription Pattern Mining}
\label{alg:dense_mining}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Transaction sequence $D$, window size $W$, threshold $\theta$, max size $k_{\max}$
\Ensure Dense patterns $\mathcal{F}$ with their intervals
\State Build item$\to$transaction map $M$
\State $\mathcal{F} \gets \emptyset$, $L_1 \gets \emptyset$
\For{each item $i \in \mathcal{A}_\ell$}
    \State $\mathcal{D}_i \gets \textsc{DenseIntervals}(M[i], W, \theta)$
    \If{$\mathcal{D}_i \neq \emptyset$}
        \State $\mathcal{F}[\{i\}] \gets \mathcal{D}_i$; $L_1 \gets L_1 \cup \{\{i\}\}$
    \EndIf
\EndFor
\For{$k = 2$ \textbf{to} $k_{\max}$}
    \State $C_k \gets \textsc{AprioriGen}(L_{k-1})$
    \State $C_k \gets \textsc{Prune}(C_k, L_{k-1})$ \Comment{Apriori property}
    \State $L_k \gets \emptyset$
    \For{each $P \in C_k$}
        \State $R \gets \bigcap_{i \in P} \mathcal{D}_i$ \Comment{Singleton interval intersection}
        \If{$R = \emptyset$} \textbf{continue} \EndIf
        \State $T_P \gets \bigcap_{i \in P} M[i]$ \Comment{Co-occurrence timestamps}
        \State $\mathcal{D}_P \gets \textsc{DenseIntervals}(T_P, W, \theta)$
        \If{$\mathcal{D}_P \neq \emptyset$}
            \State $\mathcal{F}[P] \gets \mathcal{D}_P$; $L_k \gets L_k \cup \{P\}$
        \EndIf
    \EndFor
\EndFor
\State \Return $\mathcal{F}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The \textsc{DenseIntervals} subroutine uses a cursor-based sweep with the stack-case fix for handling repeated timestamps, achieving $O(N \log N)$ per pattern.

\subsection{Regulatory Event Attribution}

\begin{definition}[Regulatory Event]
A regulatory event $r = (\text{id}, \text{type}, t_r, \mathcal{I}_r)$ has a unique identifier, type (safety\_alert, boxed\_warning, withdrawal, label\_change), timestamp $t_r$, and targeted drug set $\mathcal{I}_r \subseteq \mathcal{A}_\ell$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Density Change Score]
For pattern $P$ and event $r$ with lookback parameter $h$:
\begin{equation}
\Delta(P, r) = \bar{s}_P^{+}(r) - \bar{s}_P^{-}(r)
\label{eq:delta}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{s}_P^{-}(r)$ and $\bar{s}_P^{+}(r)$ are the mean support in the pre-event window $[t_r - h, t_r - 1]$ and post-event window $[t_r, t_r + h - 1]$, respectively.
\end{definition}

\paragraph{Statistical testing.}
For each pattern--event pair $(P, r)$, we test $H_0$: $\bar{s}_P^{-}(r) = \bar{s}_P^{+}(r)$ using Welch's $t$-test, which accommodates unequal variances between pre- and post-event periods. With $M = |\mathcal{F}| \times |\mathcal{R}|$ simultaneous hypothesis tests, we control the false discovery rate (FDR) at level $\alpha$ using the Benjamini--Hochberg procedure~\citep{benjamini1995controlling}.

\begin{definition}[Contrast Pattern Classification]
Given significance threshold $\alpha$ and change threshold $\delta$, pattern $P$ is classified relative to event $r$ as:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Disappearing}: $p < \alpha_{\text{BH}}$ and $\Delta(P, r) < -\delta$
\item \textbf{Emerging}: $p < \alpha_{\text{BH}}$ and $\Delta(P, r) > \delta$
\item \textbf{Stable}: $p \geq \alpha_{\text{BH}}$ or $|\Delta(P, r)| \leq \delta$
\end{itemize}
where $\alpha_{\text{BH}}$ is the BH-adjusted significance threshold.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Computational Complexity}

\begin{proposition}
Let $n = |\mathcal{A}_\ell|$, $N$ the number of transactions, and $|\mathcal{F}|$ the number of frequent patterns. The total complexity is:
\[
O\bigl(|\mathcal{F}|^2 + |\mathcal{F}| \cdot N \log N + |\mathcal{F}| \cdot |\mathcal{R}| \cdot h\bigr)
\]
where the three terms correspond to candidate generation, dense interval detection, and contrast analysis, respectively.
\end{proposition}

\section{Experimental Setup}\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Synthetic Prescription Data}

We generate synthetic prescription data mimicking MIMIC-IV structure. The MIMIC-IV database~\citep{johnson2023mimiciv} contains de-identified EHR data from Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center, including prescriptions, pharmacy orders, and medication administration records. Since MIMIC-IV access requires credentialing, we generate synthetic data with matched statistical properties.

\paragraph{Data generation.}
We create $N = 1{,}000$ transaction records over 19 unique ATC level-3 drug classes spanning cardiovascular (C03A--C10A), analgesic/anti-inflammatory (M01A, N02A, N02B), diabetes (A10A, A10B), antibiotic (J01C--J01M), and other categories (A02B, N06A, N05B, B01A). Individual drugs appear with probability $p_{\text{single}} = 0.35$ and co-prescription patterns (9 clinically realistic combinations) with probability $p_{\text{pattern}} = 0.20$.

\paragraph{Regulatory events.}
Two simulated FDA safety communications are embedded:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{FDA-2024-001} ($t = 333$): Boxed warning for concurrent opioid (N02A) and benzodiazepine (N05B) use; effect magnitude 0.85 (85\% reduction in co-prescription probability).
\item \textbf{FDA-2024-002} ($t = 666$): Safety alert for fluoroquinolone (J01M) tendon rupture risk; effect magnitude 0.80.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Experiments}

\paragraph{E1: Dense pattern detection.}
Mine all dense prescription patterns with $W = 30$, $\theta = 5$, and $k_{\max} = 4$. Report pattern counts by size and total dense intervals.

\paragraph{E2: Regulatory event contrast.}
For all size-2 and size-3 patterns, compute the density change score $\Delta(P, r)$ around each regulatory event with lookback $h = 100$. Apply Welch's $t$-test with BH correction at $\alpha = 0.10$. Classify patterns as disappearing, emerging, or stable.

\paragraph{E3: Parameter sensitivity.}
Vary window size $W \in \{10, 20, 30, 50, 80\}$ and threshold $\theta \in \{3, 5, 8, 10\}$ to assess parameter sensitivity.

\paragraph{E4: Multi-drug-class analysis.}
Apply the framework separately to three drug class scenarios---cardiovascular (statin hepatotoxicity warning), pain management (opioid--benzodiazepine warning), and antibiotics (fluoroquinolone warning)---each with a dedicated regulatory event.

\subsection{E3: Parameter Sensitivity}

Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity} shows the number of detected patterns as a function of window size $W$ and threshold $\theta$. \section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Clinical Implications}

Our framework provides several advantages for pharmacoepidemiological surveillance:

\paragraph{Automated multi-pattern detection.} Unlike ITS, which requires pre-specification of a single drug and intervention point, our method simultaneously monitors all co-prescription patterns and can detect unexpected cascading effects. The finding that 65.5\% of disappearing patterns were \emph{not} directly targeted by the regulatory event highlights the importance of monitoring indirect effects.

\paragraph{Contrast pattern taxonomy.} The disappearing--emerging--stable classification provides actionable intelligence for drug safety monitoring. Disappearing patterns may indicate desired prescriber behavior change; emerging patterns may signal compensatory prescribing that warrants monitoring for its own safety profile; stable patterns serve as negative controls.

\paragraph{Parameter interpretability.} The window size $W$ directly corresponds to a clinical monitoring period (e.g., $W = 30$ for monthly monitoring), and the threshold $\theta$ controls the minimum co-prescription frequency of interest.

\subsection{Limitations}

\paragraph{Synthetic data.} Our evaluation uses synthetic data rather than real MIMIC-IV prescriptions. While the data generation model captures key features (ATC-coded drugs, temporal structure, regulatory event effects), real prescription data exhibit additional complexities including seasonal patterns, provider-level variation, and indication-based confounding.

\paragraph{Confounding.} The current framework does not control for time-varying confounders such as disease prevalence changes, formulary modifications, or marketing activities that may coincide with regulatory events.

\paragraph{Scalability.} With 19 drug classes, the number of candidate patterns grows combinatorially. Larger drug vocabularies (e.g., ATC level 5 with hundreds of chemicals) would require additional pruning strategies or sampling approaches.

\paragraph{Causal interpretation.} The density change score measures association, not causation. Even with BH-corrected significance, observed changes could be due to temporal confounders rather than the regulatory event itself. Complementary causal inference methods (e.g., instrumental variables, difference-in-differences) should be applied for causal claims.

\subsection{Future Work}

Three directions are particularly promising:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Real-data validation}: Apply the framework to MIMIC-IV prescription data with documented FDA safety communications to assess performance on real EHR data.
\item \textbf{Causal attribution}: Integrate difference-in-differences or synthetic control methods to strengthen causal claims about regulatory event effects.
\item \textbf{Online monitoring}: Adapt the sliding-window approach for real-time pharmacovigilance, where new transactions arrive continuously and dense interval boundaries are updated incrementally.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented a framework for detecting dense prescription patterns and attributing density changes to regulatory events. The method combines sliding-window Apriori mining with contrast pattern analysis and FDR-controlled statistical testing. On synthetic pharmacoepidemiological data, the framework successfully identified 3{,}100 dense patterns, detected 1{,}457 significant contrast patterns around simulated FDA safety communications, and revealed cascading effects where 65.5\% of disappearing patterns were indirectly affected. The framework provides a complementary tool for pharmacovigilance that operates at the co-prescription level, offering multi-pattern surveillance capabilities not available in existing methods.
